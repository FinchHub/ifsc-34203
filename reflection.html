Final Project Reflection

Details, Feedback, and Considerations

Doxa Construction's website is meant to be the homepage of its company, primarily as both a work portfolio and a place to route potential new hires. As such, the target audience for Doxa Construction's website is new hires, as well as townships, cities, and municipalities who require water and wastewater infrastructure in the form of new construction or existing infrastructure upgrades.

When building the website, I recieved feedback from Doxa employees, including both co-founders and the office manager. Most of the advice I received was positive criticism, with all of the negatives being the quality or types of photos. Both office managers agreed that a better hero image could be used on the homepage, and that the images for the tablet/desktop views could also be switched up. This is an issue that I haven't completely addressed yet; as you navigate the website, you'll notice several photos that could be better quality or could be replaced outright. This is because my work on the website isn't finished just yet. This was a class project but it is also my first production website that I'll have made on my own, and over the next few weeks, we'll be taking many more on-site photos until we have something we're happy to put on the website.

This class and the Web Tech course weren't my first foray into webdev, but they were the first time I actually took it seriously, and I'm happy to say that I've never been more confident with my frontend skills. I've spent hours and some sleepless nights working away at this class because, thankfully, it became way more of a pasttime than just another obligation. Doxa's website was sort of my "Junior Magnum Opus" in that regard. You can find the currently existing website at doxaconstruction.com, which is not something I'm particularly proud of. When I took Web Tech last semester, the goal was to recreate the site, and althrough I think I did an okay job with the knowledge I had, I knew it just didn't really have the modern aesthetic that I wanted. This time around, I shopped on some existing construction sites like McCarthy Construction, which I took a generous amount of inspiration from when redesigning Doxa's site.

Though web development isn't my career path, I'm feeling confident and excited enough now that I've already got a few projects to continue my learning, including a portfolio site that I've been putting off for years, and a site redesign for my buddy who just put out his first book and is looking for a nicer web front. I've still got plenty to improve on and this final really tested my abilities and patience. Frankly, setting up the mobile nav was rough, and I got to find out just how easy it is to wireframe something in Figma that looks good on paper but hurts to implement. I've only got foundational knowledge with some of the more complicated things, but hopefully I'll get there.

Thanks for the amazing class and the enthusiasm you bring to it, and this field as a whole. I've been in school on and off for about five years now thanks to when I was still in the military, and this has genuinely been the most enjoyable class I've had in that time. I'll definitely miss it.
